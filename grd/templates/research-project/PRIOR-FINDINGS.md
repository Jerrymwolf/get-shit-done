<guidelines>

Apply thematic analysis principles (Braun & Clarke, 2006). Focus on WHAT the field has established empirically, grouping by theme rather than chronology.

**Thematic Clusters:**
- Group findings by what they are about, not who published them
- Each theme should capture a coherent body of evidence
- Include the evidence basis for each finding -- not just the claim
- Assess strength of evidence per theme, not per study

**Convergent Findings:**
- These are the field's most reliable conclusions
- Convergence across different methods is stronger than convergence within one method
- Note whether convergence spans different populations and contexts

**Divergent Findings:**
- Disagreement is informative -- it reveals boundary conditions
- Always include a possible explanation for divergence (method, sample, context differences)
- Divergence does not mean "we don't know" -- it means "the answer depends on..."

**Evidence Strength Assessment:**
- Volume alone is not strength -- 50 weak studies do not equal 5 strong ones
- Design quality matters: experimental > quasi-experimental > correlational > descriptive
- Consistency across methods and contexts is the strongest signal
- Be honest about weak evidence -- gaps are useful for research design

</guidelines>
